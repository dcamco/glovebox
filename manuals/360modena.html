3 · The Euro-Spec Question VALUATION FLAG

This is the single most important thing to understand about this specific car. It is a European-market 360 that was federalized and imported into the US in 2001 — not a US-delivery car.

EvidenceDetail
VIN tells youPosition 8 of the VIN is the spec marker: A = US, B = Euro. This car is ZFFYR51B — definitively Euro-spec. (A US-delivery 360 would read ZFFYR51A.)

Import trailCARFAX: imported via Miami 6/2001 + “declared to meet US highway safety specifications, Bond released” 10/2001 — the classic registered-importer / DOT-bond federalization sequence.
Alarm behaviorRO note: “vehicle is European which does not allow for beeping of the siren” when arming/disarming (2020).
Inspection quirksShop had to “drive car to get readiness codes for state inspection” (2008) — typical of a federalized ECU/emissions setup.
What it means for you
  • Value: the market discount for a Euro-spec 360 is well-documented at roughly ~$15k under a comparable US car (FerrariChat consensus) — partly regulatory, partly because some dealers won't service Euro cars (different software/parts from Europe), which narrows resale. Price it accordingly — this is leverage in negotiation, not a dealbreaker.
  • It IS properly federalized & registerable — it has passed TX safety/emissions inspection annually for ~20 years, so the import paperwork worked. Still, get the federalization / DOT-RI documents and confirm they transfer cleanly with the title.
  • Practical differences: alarm doesn't chirp; lighting and some emissions hardware are Euro-pattern; instrument cluster reads mph primary with km/h secondary (confirmed in the odometer photo — good, it's not a km-only car).
  • Parts: mechanicals are identical to a US 360; only a handful of emissions/lighting/alarm bits differ.
