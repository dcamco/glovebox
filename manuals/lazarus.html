5 · Crank Sensor & No-Start Diagnosis

Your suspected “crank sensor” is the VR (variable-reluctance) sensor that reads the 36-1 wheel — not a Land Rover sensor. It is a generic Ford-EDIS-type pickup.

What the part actually is

  • Type: 2-wire variable-reluctance (magnetic) sensor. Polarized — if the two wires are swapped, EDIS produces no spark.
  • Bracket: Trigger Wheels Rover-V8 adjustable bracket, P/N RV8-VR-Bkt (may use 5 mm spacers).
  • Sensor donor: a Ford-EDIS VR sensor. These kits are “tested with both Ford and GM VR pickups,” Ford OEM recommended. Common community donors: Ford CVH/Zetec crank sensor (Escort / Fiesta / Mondeo / Focus era), or a ’99–’01 Miata CKP — effectively any Ford VR sensor of the right body.
Do not order blind Go to the truck first. Find the 2-wire sensor pointed at the toothed wheel on the front of the crank. Read its casting/label numbers, then buy the exact match (or the cross-referenced Ford sensor). Builds differ — the markings on your sensor are the source of truth.

Bench / on-car test of the VR sensor

CheckSpec / methodBad result
Coil resistanceOhmmeter across the 2 pins. Ford-EDIS VR ≈ 300–1,400 Ω (often ~500 Ω)Open (∞) or way out of range = dead sensor
Air gapFeeler gauge, sensor tip to trigger-wheel tooth ≈ 0.5–1.5 mmToo wide = weak/erratic signal & no-start
AC outputMeter on AC volts across pins while crankingNo/low AC = sensor or wiring fault
Wiring/connectorInspect lead for heat/chafe damage; check shield/groundCommon failure near the hot engine
Trigger wheelVisual: bent/damaged teeth, debris, loose mountDamage or wobble = misfire/cutout
PolarityIf you replace/re-pin: confirm wire orientationReversed = no spark even with good sensor

5A · Active Diagnosis Log — Hot No-Start / Hot Stall OPEN · UNRESOLVED

Status: open investigation — not concluded This is a working theory, not a verdict. Root cause is NOT confirmed until the hot spark test (or a live signal measurement) is performed. Do not close this entry, and do not replace parts on assumption alone. Last update: investigation in progress — no on-truck electrical test completed yet.

Symptoms (observed by Drew)

  • Hard HOT starting, progressively worsening — cranks and cranks, eventually catches when hot; required crank time has grown steadily over time.
  • Cold starts perfect, every time — the fault is heat-gated.
  • Won't hold idle when hot — dies the moment the throttle closes.
  • Stays alive if RPM is held — drove home two-footing the throttle; let off at the destination and it died, would not restart.
  • Recovers after ~1–2 min of sitting, then fires. Continued idling never recovers it; a short cooldown does.

Leading hypothesis (unconfirmed)

Heat-marginal crank-signal amplitude in the VR → EDIS chain. A VR sensor's output voltage rises with RPM against the EDIS module's fixed trigger threshold — which accounts for every symptom: idle-death, rev-rescue, long hot cranking (lowest RPM = weakest signal), progressive worsening (a degrading part), and short-cooldown recovery (a marginal junction dropping below failure temperature).

Fuel — down-ranked, not being chased The throttle-rescue behavior is backwards for fuel starvation (a weak pump struggles most under load, not least). Cold-start-perfect plus the clean 1–2 min recovery also argue against pump/rail heat-soak. Held only as a possible separate rough-hot-idle contributor, not the no-start cause.

Suspect ranking (all heat-gated, same signal chain)

#SuspectWhy it fits / noteCost
1VR crank sensor (weak amplitude)Best fit to the RPM-dependent rev-rescue; classic heat-marginal winding/junction~£24 (TW p25) / ~$20–40 cross

Tests NOT yet done — next actions (in order)

  1. Hot spark test (the splitter): when it dies hot, check for spark while cranking. No spark hot ⇒ confirmed ignition/signal chain. Spark present ⇒ pivot to fuel / 14CUX.
  2. Measure VR signal at the sensor vs. at the EDIS module input — good at sensor / weak at module ⇒ wiring/connection; weak at both ⇒ the sensor.
  3. Voltage-drop test on the EDIS/Megajolt ground + 12V feed (hot/cranking). >~0.1–0.2 V on the ground leg = bad ground.
  4. Heat-gun resistance test of the pulled sensor: cold ≈ 300–1,400 Ω, then heat it — resistance climbing toward open = confirmed bad sensor.
  5. Freeze-spray isolation: chill only the sensor, then only the module, when it's down — whichever revives it is the culprit.
  6. Monitor RPM in the (free) Megajolt software as it dies — ragged/dropout = upstream signal; clean & steady = output/SAW side.
  7. Clean the sensor tip (inspect for ferrous debris on the magnet) + connector pins; trace the oil source (front crank seal / timing-cover leak slung by the trigger wheel). Note: oil is unlikely to be the heat-gated root cause by itself.

Parts staged (confirm by reading the installed sensor's markings first)

  • VR sensor: Trigger Wheels “Crank / Camshaft sensor,” product p25, ~£24 — matched to the RV8-VR-Bkt bracket bore. US-fast alternative: any 2-wire VR/reluctor of matching body diameter + connector.
  • Spare EDIS-8 module (Motorcraft — the module is always Ford; brand says nothing about the sensor). Cheap salvage; keep as a glovebox backup.

Resolution: PENDING. Update this log when the hot spark test or signal measurement is performed — only then assign a root cause and move the §10 issue status off OPEN.

6 · Suspension & EAS

The truck retains factory EAS (electronic air suspension). Carlos's own words on the build sheet: “stock air ride, with all eternal gremlins of EAS.”

Documented EAS history

  • CARLOS ~2010: full EAS refurb — new bags, all 4 height sensors, valve-block seals, recalibrated for heavy towing. Worked correctly.
  • CARLOS 2013 wreck aftermath: chronic problems — could not communicate with the EAS ECU using either Autologic or RSW tools; suspected the valve-block driver. Accumulated ~5 EAS ECUs, 2 drivers, a valve block chasing it. Ran on manual inflation (MARS-style, all four springs ~65 psi for near-full fixed height). Considered an AccuAir e-Level conversion (keeps compressor/reservoir/dryer).
  • NOAH reported the truck “sags in the mornings a bit” (overnight rear droop = a leak or valve-block seep). Had new rear air springs on hand to install (may or may not be fitted).
Expect to fight EAS Get a laptop EAS tool (RSW / EAS Unlock-style). Priorities: confirm ECU comms, then air springs → valve-block seals → height sensors → driver, in that order of likelihood. The AccuAir path is the documented “nuclear option” if the OEM ECU/driver won't behave.

13 · Verify on the Physical Truck

  1. Confirm displacement — 5.1 stroker vs. plain 4.6. Changes parts & expectations.
  2. Photograph & ohm the crank VR sensor + trigger wheel; read markings; then buy the matching Ford-EDIS sensor (§5).
  3. Get the Megajolt map/config file — ask Noah for any laptop files or build paperwork from Carlos.
  4. Inspect the 2013 left-front repair — structure, panel gaps, weld/filler quality.
  5. EAS health — confirm ECU comms; test the valve block & driver; install the on-hand rear springs.
  6. ARB lockers — find compressor, trace lines, confirm both engage.
  7. Rust audit — lift carpet (front floors), check mudflap posts & the “coated-not-cut” rear area.
  8. Trace the warm-idle tick — rule out exhaust-manifold leak before assuming a lifter.
