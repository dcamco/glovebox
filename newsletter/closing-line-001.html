The Week the Paperwork Got Paid

A 1979 Porsche 911SC — no low miles, no rare spec, no build — hammered for $131,000 on Bring a Trailer last Monday. Our model said $73,500 and it wasn't being timid; that was already a premium over the comps. The car's entire case was six words in the title: 43-Years-Family-Owned. The market paid roughly double spec-sheet value for provenance you could verify.

It wasn't a one-off. The same seven days: a 1,300-mile F355 GTS did $355,000 against our $188k line (we were 47% light and we'll own it below). A 27-years-family-owned 308 QV cleared 11% over its median. A 21k-mile S2000 went 67% over. Meanwhile the ordinary examples — no story, no paper — went begging: a no-history 911SC targa died at a $51,000 high bid, a 38k-mile 550 Maranello printed 11% under median, and dealer asks across the modern-Ferrari board took another week of price cuts.

The read: this market has bifurcated into two currencies — documentation and everything else. Cars that can prove their story (receipts, ownership chains, invoices naming the scary jobs) are printing 30–100% over model. Cars that merely claim one are clearing at spec-sheet value or not at all. The spread between a documented car and its undocumented twin has never been wider, and it is measurable to the dollar — see below.
Case study

Four Forecasts, One Silver Ferrari

Last Sunday a 16k-mile Euro-spec 2001 Ferrari 360 Modena F1 — Argento Nurburgring over blue, a polarizing combo — closed on BaT. Ten minutes before the hammer it sat at $83,000 and looked dead. Thirty minutes of extension sniping later it sold for $115,000.

We had four forecasts on the books, all locked before the ending — including a comment-informed re-forecast made at 3:50 that morning by a model that is never shown the current bid (it reprices on news, not on price; the bid is recorded separately purely to audit that discipline).

ForecasterLineError vs $115,000
Comment-informed closing line (masked)$113,0001.7%
AI listing-read (locked at discovery)$112,5002.2%
Deterministic comp model$111,9482.7%
Human (logged 3 days prior)$120,0004.3%

Three lessons out of one hammer. First: the Euro discount is dead — a Euro-spec car with a fumbled service question in its thread just cleared what US cars cleared in spring. Second: color is not a discount, it's a variance-widener. This car drew 7 bidders where red equivalents draw 15–18; thin pools don't lower every outcome, they make the outcome depend on who shows up. Sunday, the right two people showed up. Third: a thin ladder at T-minus-10 is not a thin pool. Snipers wait. They always wait.

The pattern in our misses is the story of the market: the models are surgical on ordinary cars (five of the last ten graded calls landed inside 3%) and structurally blind to story premiums — the family-provenance, time-capsule, last-of-its-kind lots where the crowd pays for something no spec sheet carries. We've shipped one calibration fix (anchoring trophy candidates to the top of each model's real price distribution, which cut the worst errors roughly in half in backtest) and the next issue will grade it live. The '87 Range Rover teaches the opposite lesson: comment-thread enthusiasm about an ordinary car is not a trophy signal. The crowd loved that truck right up to 40% below our excited re-forecast.
The ladder

What Three Pedals Are Worth: the Gated-360 Hierarchy, Priced

Over ten days the market priced every rung of the Ferrari 360 transmission ladder in public — a natural experiment we may never see run this cleanly again:

ConfigurationMarket evidence
Factory 6-speed, documented$245k–$302k sold (spring prints); two live lots bid $230k+ this week
Gated conversion, documented installer~$170k (best prints, both venues)
Gated conversion, unknown installerbid to $114k — reserve not met
F1 (as built), sorted$109k–$115k sold

Read the middle rows twice. The market paid essentially nothing for a conversion it couldn't verify — $114k is F1-coupe money — while attributed conversions carry a ~$60k premium over the same car with paddles. On a $25–30k conversion, the paperwork is worth more than the parts. The lot that proved it had no installer of record, no answer to "did the gearbox come out," and a single fresh belt invoice against 27 years of unknown; the thread noticed, and the bidding stopped a tier short of the reserve. In this market, invoices are a powertrain component.

The Five-360 Census

As we publish, five Ferrari 360s are live on BaT simultaneously — three factory gated cars, a conversion, and a low-mile F1 coupe. That's not a coincidence; it's a supply response to the spring repricing, and it's the first real stress test of whether the gated premium survives competition for the same deep-pocketed pool. All five are on the books with locked forecasts. Next issue grades every one — including whether the third gated car in line finds the room exhausted. If you want to know what a market top looks like, it looks like everyone deciding to sell at once.
