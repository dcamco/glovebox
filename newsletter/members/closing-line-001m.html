The gauge

The Seller Standoff Just Broke

Our reserve-gap gauge measures what fraction of no-sales had a high bid at or above the 12-month median — i.e., sellers refusing market-clearing money. Baseline runs ~27%. Through early July it spiked to 56–62%: the classic correction standoff, sellers anchored to 2024, bidders repriced, nothing trading.

Sunday's reading: 14%.

The standoff broke — and it broke in both directions at once: story cars found bids so strong that reserves stopped mattering ($131k SC, $115k Euro 360), while ordinary-car sellers began capitulating on asks (the modern-Ferrari dealer board took 40+ price cuts in three weeks; Viper asks are folding this week). When the gauge normalizes from above, the market has found its clearing level. This is the single most constructive market-structure signal since the correction began — provided you're holding the documented tier and not the ordinary one.

But Sunday's $115k silver-over-blue print taught the refinement: color is a variance-widener more than a discount. Polarizing colors thin the bidder pool (7 bidders vs red's 15–18), making the outcome attendance-dependent — the same car swings $20k on which two obsessives show up. Red doesn't outperform because red bidders pay more; it outperforms because twenty bidders make the result insensitive to any one of them. Bid polarizing colors when the room is empty; sell them only when you can summon a crowd.
